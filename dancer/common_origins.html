Diving into the urban dance scene

While I was visiting Stanford during Admit Weekend in April 2014, I was assigned to stay with a current Stanford freshman named Paul. After dropping off my stuff, I met Julian, the other ProFro (prospective freshman) who would also be crashing in Paul's room. One topic that quickly allowed the three of us to bond was dance: I had done a few school performances my senior year, Julian had a small background in breaking, and Paul was a member of Common Origins (CO), Stanford's only non-audition, urban dance team. After showing us some of their previous performances on YouTube and seeing our excited reactions, Paul invited Julian and me to a workshop CO was hosting that weekend. To put it simply, we were some of the only ProFros at the workshop, and we both got our butts kicked by an aggressive piece taught to Meek Mill's “Ooh Kill 'Em.” Despite our struggle, we still had a ton of fun and decided that when we returned to Stanford in the fall we would both join CO.

Julian (right) and I never thought we'd go from struggling at that Admit Weekend workshop to sharing the stage as teammates

Both of us kept our word and joined the fourth generation of CO dancers when we arrived back on campus in September. Fall quarter is CO's busiest time of the year since we spend it preparing for Breaking Ground, our annual dance showcase that features other on- and off-campus groups. This means that we have only ten weeks to teach ten different pieces, add transitions, create formations for our dancers, and then clean our set so that it's presentable to an audience. It was certainly a lot to take in as someone who was not only new to Stanford and CO but also relatively new to the urban dance scene. But it is for these same reasons that I experienced the fastest growth in my dance career simply because almost everything I encountered was foreign to me. Besides learning new dance terms, such as “texture” and “musicality”, I also begain teaching my body new “vocabulary,” such as hits, isolations, waves, and footwork. I discovered the difficulties that come with standardizing timing and angles for 30+ dancers, the exhiliration (and exhaustion) of late-night practices, and that crazy mix of terror, calm, and anticipation you feel as you stand on stage with the rest of your team just before the lights come on.

The fifth generation of CO dancers after Breaking Ground 2015

Perhaps most importantly of all, joining CO provided me with a group of fun, loveable people whom I would eventually call my dance family. The sense of community I felt with CO pushed me as both a dancer and team member my sophomore year. Although I did not techincally hold a leadership position that year, I furthered my growth by taking part in Choreo Days, a CO tradition that allows members to present original choreo or dance covers to the rest of the group. Doing so helped me better refine and understand my own style as a dancer while gaining experience in learning pieces off the Internet and collaborating with others. And after jumping into my first freestyle circle and experiencing the thrill of having to spontaneously create new movement, I become an avid supporter of the practice and helped organize a number of freestyle sessions for the rest of the team. As both a Co-President and a Creative Director for this coming year, I'm excited to work with the rest of my leadership team to shape CO's community and overall direction while still continuing my own personal growth.
